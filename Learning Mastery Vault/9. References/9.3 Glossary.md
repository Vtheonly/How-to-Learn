# 9.3 Glossary

Definitions of technical terms used throughout the vault. Terms are grouped by category.

## Memory and Neuroscience Terms

### Acetylcholine
A neurotransmitter released by the basal forebrain that marks synapses for retention during focused attention. Without acetylcholine release, encoding does not occur.

### Adenosine
A nucleoside that accumulates in the brain during wakefulness and produces sleep pressure. Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors, preventing the feeling of sleepiness (but not clearing adenosine).

### Amygdala
A brain region involved in emotional processing. Modulates memory encoding based on emotional salience.

### BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor)
A protein that supports synaptic plasticity, neuronal survival, and adult neurogenesis. Increased by aerobic exercise.

### Consolidation
The offline process by which fragile, hippocampus-dependent short-term memory traces are stabilized into durable neocortical long-term memory. Occurs primarily during sleep.

### Default Mode Network (DMN)
A set of brain regions (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate, angular gyrus) active during rest, mind-wandering, and self-generated thought. Anticorrelated with the central executive network.

### Hippocampus
A brain region critical for the encoding of new episodic and semantic memories. Transfers memories to the neocortex during sleep via replay.

### Long-Term Potentiation (LTP)
A sustained increase in synaptic strength following high-frequency stimulation. The cellular correlate of learning.

### Long-Term Depression (LTD)
A sustained decrease in synaptic strength. The mechanism of forgetting and competitive pruning.

### Neocortex
The outer layer of the brain, where long-term memories are stored after consolidation.

### Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to physically rewire itself (forming, strengthening, or pruning synapses) in response to experience.

### Norepinephrine
A neurotransmitter released by the locus coeruleus that provides arousal and alertness. Gates neuroplasticity.

### Prefrontal Cortex
The brain region responsible for executive control, complex logic, and system design. Reaches full maturity around age 25.

### Reconsolidation
The process by which retrieving a memory destabilizes it briefly, then re-stores it (usually stronger). The mechanism behind the testing effect.

### REM Sleep
Rapid eye movement sleep. The brain is highly active; the body is paralyzed. Dominates the second half of the night. Critical for emotional and procedural memory consolidation.

### Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS)
Deep sleep characterized by slow delta waves. Dominates the first half of the night. Critical for declarative memory consolidation via hippocampal replay.

### Synaptogenesis
The formation of new synapses. Most active in childhood but continues in adults (especially in the hippocampus).

### Vigilance Decrement
The natural decline in attention quality over time during a sustained task. Begins after 20-40 minutes for most tasks.

## Cognitive Psychology Terms

### Active Recall
A learning technique in which the learner forces information out of memory rather than passively re-exposing themselves to it. Also called the testing effect or retrieval practice.

### Atomic Note
A note that contains exactly one concept or one fact. The atomic unit of a knowledge base.

### Cognitive Load
The total amount of mental effort being used in working memory. Divided into intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load.

### Completion Method
A learning technique in which the learner completes partial worked examples rather than writing solutions from scratch.

### Elaborative Interrogation
A learning technique in which the learner asks "why?" about each fact, integrating new information with existing schemas.

### Fluency Illusion
The subjective feeling that one understands material, based on the ease of processing. Often misleads learners into believing they have learned when they have only recognized.

### Forgetting Curve
The predictable decay of memory over time without review. Documented by Ebbinghaus (1885).

### Free Recall
A retrieval technique in which the learner writes down everything they can remember about a topic, without cues or structure.

### Germane Load
Cognitive load devoted to schema construction. The "productive" load that produces learning.

### Hypercorrection Effect
The finding that high-confidence errors produce stronger subsequent learning than low-confidence errors. Because the surprise is maximal when one is confidently wrong.

### Illusion of Competence
The false belief that one has mastered material, based on recognition fluency or superficial familiarity.

### Intrinsic Load
The inherent difficulty of material, determined by element interactivity (the number of elements that must be processed simultaneously).

### Leeche
In spaced repetition, a card that the learner fails repeatedly (typically 6+ lapses). Indicates a poorly designed card or an unlearned concept.

### Mind Map
A visual note-taking technique in which a central concept is placed in the middle of a page, with related ideas branching outward radially.

### MOC (Map of Content)
An index note that serves as a navigation hub for a topic or chapter. Lists and links to related atomic notes.

### Parsons Problem
A programming puzzle in which scrambled code blocks must be rearranged into the correct logical sequence.

### Pomodoro Technique
A time-management method using 25-minute focus intervals separated by 5-minute breaks.

### Pretesting
The practice of attempting questions on material before studying it. Produces stronger subsequent learning via the hypercorrection effect.

### Recognition vs. Recall
Recognition is the feeling of familiarity when re-exposed to information. Recall is the ability to produce the information from memory. Recognition is much easier and is a poor proxy for recall.

### Retrograde Interference
The phenomenon where newly learned information disrupts the consolidation of previously learned information.

### Retrieval Practice
See Active Recall.

### Schema
A mental pattern representing common structures (e.g., the "accumulator" pattern in programming). Schemas enable pattern recognition and efficient processing.

### Scatter Focus
The deliberate practice of mind-wandering during low-stimulation activities. Activates the Default Mode Network.

### Spaced Repetition
The systematic scheduling of review sessions at expanding intervals, designed so each review occurs just before the memory trace decays.

### Stop and Recite
A micro-retrieval technique in which the learner pauses every 10 minutes during reading to recite key points from memory.

### Testing Effect
See Active Recall.

### Worked Example
A complete, step-by-step solution to a problem, studied by the learner as a model.

## Computer Science Education Terms

### Code Tracing
The act of mentally executing a piece of code step-by-step, tracking variable values at each step.

### Completion Method
See Cognitive Psychology Terms.

### Cognitive Load Theory (CLT)
A framework distinguishing intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load on working memory. Used to design effective instruction.

### Notional Machine
An abstract, simplified model of how a computer executes programs. The mental model a programmer uses to predict code behavior.

### Parsons Problem
See Cognitive Psychology Terms.

## Time Management and Productivity Terms

### 2-Minute Rule
A productivity rule: if a task takes less than 2 minutes, do it immediately rather than adding it to a to-do list.

### Attention Residue
The persistence of thoughts about a previous task after switching to a new one. Reduces cognitive capacity for the new task.

### Diffuse Mode
A cognitive state characterized by broad, undirected attention and activation of the Default Mode Network. Supports insight and consolidation.

### First Three Steps Rule
A productivity rule: when facing a large task, identify only the first three concrete steps. Do not plan beyond them.

### Flow
A state of deep, effortless concentration on a challenging task. Coined by Csikszentmihalyi.

### Focus Mode
A cognitive state characterized by narrow, directed attention and activation of the central executive network.

### Hyperfocus
An intense form of focused attention, often to the exclusion of everything else. Can be productive (deep work) or destructive (doomscrolling).

### Novelty Bias
The brain's built-in tendency to capture attention by novel stimuli. Exploited by social media and notification systems.

### Pomodoro Technique
See Cognitive Psychology Terms.

### Time-Based Goal
A goal defined in terms of time spent ("study for 90 minutes") rather than outcome ("finish chapter 4"). Reduces anxiety and encourages depth.

## Brainwave and Sleep Terms

### Brainwaves
Oscillating electrical patterns in the brain, measured by EEG. Different frequencies are associated with different cognitive states.

### Delta Waves
Slow (0.5-4 Hz) brainwaves characteristic of deep slow-wave sleep.

### Gamma Waves
Fast (30-100 Hz) brainwaves associated with focused attention and integration of information. The 40 Hz gamma therapy myth is debunked in [[7.2 Biohacking Myths]].

### REM Sleep
See Memory and Neuroscience Terms.

### Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS)
See Memory and Neuroscience Terms.

### Sleep Spindles
Brief bursts of brain activity during NREM Stage 2 sleep. Linked to motor skill consolidation.

### Ultradian Rhythm
A biological rhythm shorter than 24 hours. The 90-minute sleep cycle is a real ultradian rhythm. The "90-minute waking focus cycle" is a myth; see [[7.2 Biohacking Myths]].

## Tool and Workflow Terms

### Anki
The dominant spaced repetition software. Open-source, cross-platform, free (except iOS).

### Atomic Note
See Cognitive Psychology Terms.

### Local-First
A software design principle in which data is stored locally (in plain files) and synced optionally. Contrast with cloud-locked.

### Markdown
A plain-text format for writing structured documents. Used by Obsidian, Logseq, and many other tools.

### MOC
See Cognitive Psychology Terms.

### Obsidian
A local-first markdown note-taking tool with bidirectional linking and a large plugin ecosystem.

### REMNote
A combined note-taking and spaced repetition tool.

### SM-2 Algorithm
The spaced repetition algorithm used by Anki. Schedules review intervals based on past performance.

### Wiki Link
A link in the form `[[Note Title]]` that creates a bidirectional connection between notes. Used in Obsidian, Logseq, and other knowledge tools.
