# Glossary Index

> Definitions for every cognitive term used in the vault. One term per entry; entries are alphabetical.

---

## A

### Adaptive Expertise
The ability to invent novel solutions when familiar schemas do not fit, built through deliberate exposure to novelty. Contrast with **routine expertise**. See [[01_Theory/T8 — Adaptive Expertise|T8]].

### Advance Organizer
An introductory framework presented before new material that activates relevant prior knowledge. Related to Ausubel's meaningful learning theory.

### Analogical Encoding
Learning multiple examples that share an underlying schema, which facilitates abstraction and transfer. See [[03_Methods/M6 — Analogical Comparison|M6]].

### Analogical Reasoning
The cognitive process of mapping relational structure from a familiar "base" domain to a novel "target" domain. See [[01_Theory/T1 — Schema Transfer|T1]].

### Attribute Commonality
Shared surface features between two domains. Does NOT drive transfer; contrast with **relational commonality**.

### ACT-R
Anderson's cognitive architecture: knowledge stored as declarative chunks + production rules.

---

## B

### Backward Design
Curriculum design starting from desired outcomes, working backward to activities. Wiggins & McTighe. See [[08_References/References Index|References]].

### Blocked Practice
Studying one topic extensively before moving to the next. Generally inferior to **interleaved practice** for retention and transfer.

### Bloom's 2-Sigma Problem
The finding that one-on-one tutoring produces ~2 standard deviation improvement over classroom instruction. The "problem" is how to achieve this at scale.

---

## C

### Cognitive Load Theory (CLT)
Sweller's framework distinguishing intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load on working memory. See [[01_Theory/T2 — Cognitive Load Theory|T2]].

### Concept-Based Curriculum
Curriculum organized around transferable concepts rather than domain-specific topics. Erickson.

### Concept Mapping
Drawing a graph where nodes are concepts and edges are labeled with relation types. See [[03_Methods/M9 — Concept Mapping|M9]].

### Cone of Experience
Edgar Dale's (mis)representation of learning modalities. Largely discredited; not used in this vault.

### Congruent Association
Two pieces of information encoded together such that one cues the other. Strengthens retrieval.

### Consciousness
Not addressed in this vault. The vault operates at the level of cognitive mechanisms, not philosophical questions.

### Contrastive Case
An example that looks similar to a target schema but differs in a crucial way. Used to sharpen schema boundaries.

### Convergent Evidence
Multiple independent lines of research supporting the same conclusion. The vault's recommendations all have convergent evidence.

### Creative Thinking
See [[01_Theory/T8 — Adaptive Expertise|Adaptive Expertise]].

---

## D

### Declarative Knowledge
Knowledge of facts and concepts ("what"). Contrasted with procedural knowledge ("how"). In ACT-R, stored as declarative chunks.

### Deliberate Practice
Ericsson's framework: focused, feedback-rich, repeatable practice at the edge of ability, targeting specific sub-skills. See [[01_Theory/T3 — Deliberate Practice|T3]].

### Desirable Difficulty
A condition that slows initial learning but improves long-term retention and transfer. See [[01_Theory/T6 — Desirable Difficulties|T6]].

### Distributed Practice
See **Spaced Repetition**.

### Dreyfus Model
5-stage expertise development: Novice → Advanced Beginner → Competent → Proficient → Expert. See [[01_Theory/T8 — Adaptive Expertise|T8]].

---

## E

### Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve
The exponential decay of memory strength over time without review. Empirical basis for spaced repetition.

### Elaboration
Connecting new information to prior knowledge through explanation, example generation, or analogical mapping. See [[03_Methods/M5 — Elaboration & Self-Explanation|M5]].

### Element Interactivity
The number of elements that must be processed simultaneously to understand a concept. High element interactivity = high intrinsic load. See [[01_Theory/T2 — Cognitive Load Theory|T2]].

### Expertise Reversal Effect
Methods that help novices (e.g., worked examples) can hurt experts because the scaffolding becomes extraneous load. Kalyuga 2007. See [[01_Theory/T5 — Expert-Novice Differences|T5]].

### Expert-Novice Differences
Differences in how experts and novices classify, represent, and solve problems. Experts classify by deep structure; novices by surface features. See [[01_Theory/T5 — Expert-Novice Differences|T5]].

---

## F

### 4C/ID Model
Four-Component Instructional Design (van Merriënboer). For complex skills: recurrent skills, non-recurrent skills, just-in-time info, part-task/whole-task practice.

### Far Transfer
Transfer across different context and content. Rare without explicit cuing. Contrast with **near transfer**.

### Feynman Technique
Explaining a concept in plain language as a self-test. Surfaces gaps in understanding.

### Forward Reasoning
Experts' strategy: recognize the problem type, retrieve the schema, apply it forward. Contrast with **means-ends analysis**.

---

## G

### Generation Effect
Information the learner **generates** is better remembered than information the learner reads. Slamecka & Graf 1978. See [[01_Theory/T7 — Generative Learning|T7]].

### Generative Learning
Wittrock's theory: learning requires the learner to actively construct relationships between prior knowledge and new information. See [[01_Theory/T7 — Generative Learning|T7]].

### Germane Load
Cognitive resources devoted to schema construction and automation. Should be maximized, not minimized. See [[01_Theory/T2 — Cognitive Load Theory|T2]].

---

## I

### Implementation Intention
An if-then plan ("When X happens, I will do Y"). Gollwitzer. See [[03_Methods/M7 — Implementation Intentions|M7]].

### Interleaving
Mixing problem types within a study session instead of blocking. Improves discrimination. See [[03_Methods/M3 — Interleaving|M3]].

### Intrinsic Load
Cognitive load inherent to the material's difficulty (element interactivity). Cannot be reduced without changing what is learned. See [[01_Theory/T2 — Cognitive Load Theory|T2]].

---

## K

### Knowledge Compilation
The ACT-R process by which declarative knowledge becomes procedural (production rules) through practice.

### Knowledge Graph
A graph where nodes are concepts and edges are relations. In this vault, the schema dossiers + concept notes form a knowledge graph.

---

## L

### Long-Term Memory (LTM)
Memory with effectively unlimited capacity and duration. The substrate of expertise.

### Long-Term Working Memory (LT-WM)
Ericsson & Kintsch's construct: retrieval structures in LTM that allow experts to bypass WM limits. See [[01_Theory/T4 — Long-Term Working Memory|T4]].

---

## M

### Means-Ends Analysis
Novices' strategy: work backward from the goal. Consumes WM; prevents schema encoding. Contrast with **forward reasoning**.

### Mental Model
An organized representation in LTM that supports reasoning about a domain.

### Metacognition
Thinking about one's own thinking. Includes monitoring ("do I understand this?") and control ("should I reread?"). Often poorly calibrated in novices.

---

## N

### Near Transfer
Transfer within the same context, different content. Robust. Contrast with **far transfer**.

---

## P

### Pattern Recognition
The expert's ability to rapidly identify the schema a problem instantiates. Built through deliberate practice.

### Productive Failure
Kapur's framework: failing at a problem before instruction produces better transfer than direct instruction. See [[01_Theory/T8 — Adaptive Expertise|T8]].

### Production Rule
An if-then condition-action pair. The procedural knowledge format in ACT-R.

### Programming Plans
Soloway & Ehrlich's term for reusable code schemas possessed by expert programmers.

### Progressive Abstraction
The pedagogical technique of starting with concrete examples and gradually abstracting to the schema.

---

## R

### Recognition-Primed Decision Making (RPD)
Klein's model: experts recognize a situation as similar to past situations and apply the corresponding action. Based on pattern matching, not analysis.

### Relational Commonality
Shared causal or logical structure between two domains. Drives transfer. Contrast with **attribute commonality**.

### Retrieval Practice
Pulling information out of memory rather than re-reading. Strongest single study method known. See [[03_Methods/M1 — Retrieval Practice|M1]].

### Retrieval Structure
An organized LTM pattern that allows rapid retrieval of specific items via cues. The substrate of LT-WM.

### Routine Expertise
The ability to solve familiar problems efficiently. Contrast with **adaptive expertise**.

---

## S

### Schema
An organized knowledge structure abstracted from multiple instances of the same relational pattern. See [[01_Theory/T1 — Schema Transfer|T1]].

### Schema Abstraction
The process of extracting common relational structure from multiple examples.

### Schema Theory
The body of theory describing how schemas form, organize, and drive transfer.

### Self-Explanation
Generating explanations for oneself during learning. Fiorella & Mayer. See [[03_Methods/M5 — Elaboration & Self-Explanation|M5]].

### Skilled Memory Theory
Gobet & Simon's extension of chunking theory: experts use larger templates (multi-chunk patterns) in LTM.

### SOI Model
Mayer's Selecting-Organizing-Integrating model of meaningful learning. See [[01_Theory/T7 — Generative Learning|T7]].

### Spaced Repetition
Distributing reviews over expanding intervals. The optimal gap ≈ 10–20% of the desired retention interval. See [[03_Methods/M2 — Spaced Repetition|M2]].

### Split-Attention Effect
Extraneous load created when learners must integrate information from multiple sources (e.g., text + separate diagram).

### Strategic Triage
The practice of deciding what to learn deeply, skim, defer, or ignore. See [[03_Methods/M10 — Strategic Triage|M10]] and [[04_Protocols/P6 — How to Triage What to Ignore|P6]].

### Structure Mapping Theory
Gentner's theory of analogical reasoning: transfer via structural alignment between base and target domains. See [[01_Theory/T1 — Schema Transfer|T1]].

### Systematicity Principle
Gentner's principle: higher-order relations (relations between relations) transfer more robustly than first-order relations.

---

## T

### Testing Effect
The finding that retrieval practice produces better retention than re-study. Roediger & Karpicke 2006. See [[03_Methods/M1 — Retrieval Practice|M1]].

### Threshold Concept
A concept that, once understood, transformss the learner's view of the domain. Meyer & Land.

### Transfer of Learning
The application of knowledge learned in one context to a different context. See [[01_Theory/T1 — Schema Transfer|T1]].

---

## W

### Worked Example
A fully solved problem presented for study. Reduces extraneous load; especially effective for novices. See [[03_Methods/M4 — Worked Examples|M4]].

### Working Memory (WM)
The cognitive system that holds and manipulates ~4 chunks for ~30 seconds. The bottleneck of learning. See [[01_Theory/T2 — Cognitive Load Theory|T2]].
