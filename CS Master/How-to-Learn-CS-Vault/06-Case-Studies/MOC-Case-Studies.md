# 👥 MOC — Case Studies

> *Qualitative workflows of historically prolific CS pioneers.*

---

## The Question

> *Are there empirical studies, qualitative research, or biographical analyses of historically prolific computer scientists that document their exact daily/weekly reading, note-taking, implementation, and knowledge-management workflows?*

Yes — though the literature is sparser than for, say, mathematicians or physicists. CS as a field is young, and biographical scholarship lags. What we have:

- Biographical memoirs (Knuth, McCarthy, Dijkstra)
- Self-documented workflows (Norvig's "Teach Yourself Programming in 10 Years")
- Cultural accounts of research environments (Bell Labs, Xerox PARC, MIT AI Lab)
- Modern practitioner reflections (Karpathy, tptacek, Victor)

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

Reading these workflows, common patterns emerge:

1. **Sustained daily reading and writing.** All of them wrote daily. None relied on "inspiration."
2. **Reading across the field, not just one's specialty.** Knuth reads math, music, theology; Dijkstra read physics, philosophy; Norvig reads widely across CS.
3. **Implementation as thinking.** Knuth writes code with the book in mind; Dijkstra wrote manuscripts *as* the thinking; McCarthy built Lisp to think about AI.
4. **Strong opinions about notation.** All of them care deeply about how things are written. Notation is a cognitive tool.
5. **Long time horizons.** Knuth: 50+ years on TAOCP. Dijkstra: decades of EWDs. Norvig: 10+ years before "expert."
6. **Limited use of "productivity" tools.** They use paper, simple text files, focused environments.
7. **Teaching as a learning method.** All of them taught, and the act of teaching forced schema clarity.

---

## How to Use These Case Studies

Don't try to copy any one workflow. Instead:

1. Read each case study
2. Extract 2-3 patterns that resonate
3. Test them in your own practice for 2-4 weeks
4. Keep what works; drop what doesn't
5. Repeat with the next case study

The goal is to assemble your own workflow from the components that work for *you*, not to imitate anyone.
