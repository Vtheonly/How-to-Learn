# 🚪 Start Here

Welcome. This vault is a **learning operating system** for computer science. It assumes you are serious about becoming a deep practitioner in 3–7 years, not a casual hobbyist.

---

## What This Vault Is

A structured, research-grounded guide to **how high-velocity technical learners actually work** — translated into protocols you can run today.

It draws on:

- **Cognitive Load Theory** (Sweller, 1988–present)
- **Long-Term Working Memory** (Ericsson & Kintsch, 1995)
- **Schema Theory** (Rumelhart, 1980; Anderson, 1977)
- **Structure Mapping Theory** (Gentner, 1983)
- **Cognitive Flexibility Theory** (Spiro, 1988)
- **Expert Performance Research** (Ericsson, Krampe, Tesch-Römer, 1993)
- **Testing Effect / Retrieval Practice** (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006)
- **Threshold Concepts** (Meyer & Land, 2003)

Plus qualitative studies of CS pioneers (Knuth, Dijkstra, McCarthy, Bell Labs engineers, Norvig).

See [[Bibliography]] for full citations.

## The Three Commitments

Before you proceed, commit to:

1. **Daily deliberate practice** — see [[Deliberate-Practice]]. Minimum 90 minutes of focused, hard work on the edge of your competence, most days.
2. ** Ruthless triage** — see [[Selective-Ignorance]]. You will *not* read everything. You will read the right things deeply.
3. **Build to consolidate** — see [[Build-to-Learn]]. Every major concept ends in a working artifact.

If you cannot commit to these, this vault will not help you.

## The First Lesson

> **Learning is not the act of being exposed to information. It is the act of restructuring your internal schemas so that information becomes retrievable and actionable.**

Reading 1000 pages without schema integration is *worse* than reading 100 pages with integration, because the unstructured information clogs your working memory and crowds out future learning.

This is the single most important idea in the vault. Everything else is technique.
