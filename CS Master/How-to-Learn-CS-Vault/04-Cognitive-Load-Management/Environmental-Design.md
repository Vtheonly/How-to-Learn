# Environmental Design

> *Your environment is the largest single source of extraneous cognitive load.*

---

## Principle

Willpower is a poor strategy for focus. Environment design is reliable. If you need willpower to focus, your environment is wrong.

The goal: make focus the default and distraction require effort.

---

## The Six Environmental Factors

### 1. Single-tasking device

- One monitor, one window, one application
- Close all other tabs
- Full-screen mode
- No notifications (OS-level: do-not-disturb on)
- Phone in another room (not on desk, not in pocket)

If you can't work without checking your phone in <30 min, you have a dopamine regulation problem. Fix that first.

### 2. Physical space

- Same space every session (creates context-dependent retrieval cue)
- Clean desk (visual clutter = extraneous load)
- Comfortable chair, proper monitor height
- Adequate lighting (low light = fatigue)
- Stable temperature (cold/hot = distraction)

### 3. Auditory environment

- Silence is best for hard material
- Brown noise or white noise is OK
- Music without lyrics is OK for familiar material
- Music with lyrics is *never* OK for hard material (lyrics compete for verbal processing)
- Podcasts are *never* OK during deep work

### 4. Temporal environment

- Same time of day for deep work (circadian alignment)
- Morning is typically best (cortisol peak, fewer accumulated distractions)
- Don't check email/social media before first deep block (priming distraction)
- Don't break deep work with meetings

### 5. Social environment

- Communicate your schedule to people who might interrupt
- "Deep work 9-11, do not interrupt unless emergency"
- Use a visible signal (closed door, headphones, status light)
- Find an accountability partner or community (sustains motivation, provides feedback)

### 6. Digital environment

- Block distracting sites at the OS level (Cold Turkey, Freedom, LeechBlock)
- Use a separate user account for deep work (no Slack, no email client installed)
- Use a distraction-free editor (Obsidian, Vim, Emacs in fullscreen)
- Disable all non-essential notifications permanently

---

## Protocol: The Pre-Session Environment Check

Before each deep work block (2 min):

1. [ ] Phone in another room or powered off
2. [ ] Notifications off (OS-level DND)
3. [ ] All non-essential tabs closed
4. [ ] Distraction blocker running
5. [ ] Water/tea within reach
6. [ ] Bathroom used
7. [ ] Door closed / signal visible
8. [ ] Same physical position as last session

This sounds obsessive. It's not. Each item you skip adds extraneous load. Skipping all 8 makes deep work nearly impossible.

---

## The First 5 Minutes

The hardest part of deep work is the transition. The first 5 minutes feel uncomfortable — your brain wants the easy dopamine of distraction.

**Push through the first 5 minutes.** Within 10 min, you'll be in flow. Within 20, you'll have forgotten you wanted to check your phone.

If you can't get through the first 5 minutes consistently, your environment is wrong (or you have a dopamine regulation problem). Fix the environment first.

---

## The Distraction Audit

Every 2 weeks, audit your last 14 sessions:

- How many were interrupted by an external trigger?
- How many were interrupted by an internal trigger (urge to check something)?
- What was the most common trigger?

Address the most common trigger first. Iterate.

---

## Common Anti-Patterns

- ❌ "I can focus with the TV on" (you can't; you're just not noticing the load)
- ❌ "I'll check Twitter just for a sec" (the sec becomes 20 min)
- ❌ "Music helps me focus" (only true for familiar material; never for hard material)
- ❌ "I work better in coffee shops" (you may enjoy it more; you don't work better)
- ❌ "Notifications are fine, I just don't check them" (the *interruption* of the notification itself costs ~23 min to fully recover from — Gloria Mark, UC Irvine)
- ❌ "Open office is fine" (it's not; research is unambiguous)
