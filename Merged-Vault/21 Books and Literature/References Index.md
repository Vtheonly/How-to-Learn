# References Index

> Annotated bibliography. Organized by topic. Every citation in the vault resolves here.

## Schema Transfer & Analogical Reasoning

- **Gentner, D. (1983).** Structure-Mapping: A Theoretical Framework for Analogy. *Cognitive Science, 7*(2), 155–170.
  - Foundational paper on analogical reasoning. Transfer depends on relational structure, not surface features. The **systematicity principle**: higher-order relations transfer more robustly than first-order relations.
  - Cited in: [[01_Theory/T1 — Schema Transfer|T1]], [[03_Methods/M6 — Analogical Comparison|M6]].

- **Gick, M. L., & Holyoak, K. J. (1983).** Schema Induction and Analogical Transfer. *Cognitive Psychology, 15*(1), 1–38.
  - Participants who **compared** two analogous stories spontaneously induced an abstract schema and transferred it. Single-story groups did not. **Comparison is the active ingredient.**
  - Cited in: [[01_Theory/T1 — Schema Transfer|T1]].

- **Barnett, S. M., & Ceci, S. J. (2002).** When and Where Do We Apply What We Learn? A Taxonomy for Far Transfer. *Psychological Bulletin, 128*(4), 612–637.
  - Taxonomy distinguishing near from far transfer along 6 dimensions. Far transfer is rare without explicit cuing.
  - Cited in: [[01_Theory/T1 — Schema Transfer|T1]].

- **Perkins, D. N., & Salomon, G. (1992).** Transfer of Learning. *International Encyclopedia of Education* (2nd ed.).
  - Distinguishes "low-road" (automatization-driven) from "high-road" (abstraction-driven) transfer.
  - Cited in: [[01_Theory/T1 — Schema Transfer|T1]].

- **Holyoak, K. J., & Thagard, P. (1995).** *Mental Leaps: Analogy in Creative Thought.* MIT Press.
  - Book-length treatment of analogical reasoning as a core cognitive mechanism.

- **Kurtz, K. J., Miao, C.-H., & Gentner, D. (2001).** Learning by Analogical Bootstrapping. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 130*(3), 449–467.
  - Direct experimental evidence that **comparing** examples produces stronger schema abstraction than studying them separately.

## ACT-R & Production Rule Theories

- **Anderson, J. R. (1983).** *The Architecture of Cognition.* Harvard University Press.
  - Original ACT-R theory. Knowledge stored as declarative chunks + production rules.

- **Anderson, J. R., & Lebiere, C. (1998).** *The Atomic Components of Thought.* Erlbaum.
  - Modern ACT-R. Production rules as the substrate of transfer.

- **Anderson, J. R., & Reder, L. M. (1999).** The Size of the Fan Effect: Process Not Represented. *American Journal of Psychology, 112*(3), 379–404.
  - Experimental evidence that transfer occurs when production rule conditions match structurally.

## Schema Theory & Memory Schemas

- **Bartlett, F. C. (1932).** *Remembering.* Cambridge University Press.
  - Origin of "schema" in cognitive psychology.

- **Ghosh, V. E., & Gilboa, A. (2014).** What Is a Memory Schema? A Historical Perspective on Current Neuroscience Literature. *Neuropsychologia, 53*, 104–114.

- **Gilboa, A., & Marlatte, H. (2017).** Neurobiology of Schemas and Schema-Mediated Memory. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 21*(8), 618–631.
  - Review of neuroscience evidence that schemas influence encoding, consolidation, and retrieval.

- **Preston, A. R., & Eichenbaum, H. (2013).** Interplay of Hippocampus and Prefrontal Cortex in Memory. *Current Biology, 23*(17), R764–R773.

## Cognitive Load, Working Memory, Chunking

- **Sweller, J. (1988).** Cognitive Load During Problem Solving: Effects on Learning. *Cognitive Science, 12*(2), 257–285.
  - Original Cognitive Load Theory paper.

- **Sweller, J., van Merriënboer, J. J. G., & Paas, F. (2019).** Cognitive Architecture and Instructional Design: 20 Years Later. *Educational Psychology Review, 31*(2), 261–292.
  - Modern synthesis. Distinguishes intrinsic, extraneous, germane load.

- **Sweller, J., & Cooper, G. A. (1985).** The Use of Worked Examples as a Substitute for Problem Solving in Learning Algebra. *Cognition and Instruction, 2*(1), 59–89.
  - The worked-example effect.

- **Kalyuga, S. (2007).** Expertise Reversal Effect and Its Implications for Learner-Tailored Instruction. *Educational Psychology Review, 19*(4), 509–539.
  - Methods that help novices can hurt experts. Critical for adaptive instruction.

- **van Merriënboer, J. J. G. (1997).** *Training Complex Cognitive Skills.* Educational Technology Publications.
  - The 4C/ID model for complex learning.

- **Cowan, N. (2001).** The Magical Number 4 in Short-Term Memory. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 24*(1), 87–114.
  - Modern estimate of working memory capacity: ~4 chunks.

- **Miller, G. A. (1956).** The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two. *Psychological Review, 63*(2), 81–97.
  - Classic but loose upper bound on working memory.

- **Peterson, L. R., & Peterson, M. J. (1959).** Short-Term Retention of Individual Verbal Items. *Journal of Experimental Psychology, 58*(3), 193–198.
  - Working memory decay: ~15–30 seconds without rehearsal.

## Long-Term Working Memory & Expert Memory

- **Ericsson, K. A., & Kintsch, W. (1995).** Long-Term Working Memory. *Psychological Review, 102*(2), 211–245.
  - The original LT-WM paper. Experts bypass WM limits via LTM retrieval structures.

- **Chase, W. G., & Simon, H. A. (1973).** Perception in Chess. *Cognitive Psychology, 4*(1), 55–81.
  - Master chess memory for real positions vs. random boards.

- **Gobet, F., & Simon, H. A. (1998).** Expert Chess Memory: Revisiting the 4-Chunk Hypothesis. *Memory, 6*(2), 161–193.
  - Template theory: experts use larger LTM templates, not just 4 chunks.

- **de Groot, A. D. (1965).** *Thought and Choice in Chess.* Mouton.
  - Foundational study of chess expertise.

- **Ericsson, K. A., & Polson, P. G. (1988).** An Experimental Analysis of a Waiter's Memory. *Cognitive Psychology, 20*(1), 38–71.
  - Domain-specific LT-WM in a waiter.

## Expertise & Deliberate Practice

- **Ericsson, K. A., Krampe, R. T., & Tesch-Römer, C. (1993).** The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert Performance. *Psychological Review, 100*(3), 363–406.
  - The seminal deliberate-practice paper.

- **Hambrick, D. Z., Oswald, F. L., Altmann, E. M., Meinz, E. J., Gobet, F., & Campitelli, G. (2014).** Deliberate Practice: Is That All It Takes to Become an Expert? *Intelligence, 45*, 34–45.
  - Deliberate practice explains 30–50% of variance; not sufficient alone.

- **Soloway, E., & Ehrlich, K. (1984).** Empirical Studies of Programming Knowledge. *Communications of the ACM, 27*(5), 459–474.
  - Expert programmers possess reusable "programming plans."

- **Hatano, G., & Inagaki, K. (1986).** Two Courses of Expertise. In *Child Development and Education in Japan.* Freeman.
  - Distinction between routine and adaptive expertise.

- **Bransford, J., Brown, A. L., & Cocking, R. R. (Eds.) (2000).** *How People Learn.* National Academies Press.
  - NRC synthesis of learning sciences.

## Expert-Novice Differences

- **Chi, M. T. H., Feltovich, P. J., & Glaser, R. (1981).** Categorization and Representation of Physics Problems by Experts and Novices. *Cognitive Science, 5*(2), 121–152.
  - Novices sort by surface; experts by deep structure.

- **Adelson, B. (1981).** Problem Solving and the Development of Abstract Categories in Programming Languages. *Memory & Cognition, 9*(4), 422–433.
  - Expert programmers recall by function; novices by syntax.

- **Norman, G. R., Eva, K. W., & Brooks, L. R. (2006).** Expertise in Medicine. In *The Cambridge Handbook of Expertise.* Cambridge University Press.

## Retrieval, Spacing, Interleaving, Desirable Difficulties

- **Roediger, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006).** Test-Enhanced Learning. *Psychological Science, 17*(3), 249–255.
  - The testing effect. Tested group remembered ~50% a week later vs. ~35% for re-study.

- **Karpicke, J. D., & Roediger, H. L. (2008).** The Critical Importance of Retrieval for Learning. *Science, 319*(5865), 966–968.

- **Cepeda, N. J., Pashler, H., Vul, E., Wixted, J. T., & Rohrer, D. (2006).** Distributed Practice in Verbal Recall Tasks: A Review and Quantitative Synthesis. *Psychological Bulletin, 132*(3), 354–380.
  - Meta-analysis of spacing. Optimal gap ≈ 10–20% of retention interval.

- **Brunmair, M., & Richter, T. (2019).** Similarity Matters: A Meta-Analysis of Interleaved Learning and Its Moderators. *Psychological Bulletin, 145*(9), 1029–1052.
  - Interleaving works best when items share a schema family.

- **Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007).** The Shuffling of Mathematics Problems Improves Learning. *Instructional Science, 35*(6), 481–498.

- **Bjork, R. A., & Bjork, E. L. (2011).** Making Things Hard on Yourself, But in a Good Way: Creating Desirable Difficulties. In *Successful Remembering and Successful Forgetting.* Psychology Press.

- **Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T. (2013).** Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques. *Psychological Science in the Public Interest, 14*(1), 4–58.
  - Systematic review. Practice testing and distributed practice rated "high utility"; highlighting and rereading rated "low utility."

## Generative Learning, Self-Explanation, Elaboration

- **Slamecka, N. J., & Graf, P. (1978).** The Generation Effect. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Learning and Memory, 4*(6), 592–604.
  - Original generation effect paper.

- **Wittrock, M. C. (1989).** Generative Processes of Comprehension. *Educational Psychologist, 24*(4), 345–376.

- **Mayer, R. E. (1996).** Learning Strategies for Making Sense out of Expository Text: The SOI Model for Guiding Three Cognitive Processes in Knowledge Construction. *Educational Psychology Review, 8*(4), 357–371.
  - Selecting-Organizing-Integrating model.

- **Fiorella, L., & Mayer, R. E. (2015).** Learning as a Generative Activity: Eight Learning Strategies That Promote Understanding. Cambridge University Press.
  - Book-length treatment. Self-explanation, drawing, teaching, imagining, summarizing all rated as generative.

- **Atkinson, R. K., Derry, S. J., Renkl, A., & Wortham, D. (2000).** Learning from Examples. *Review of Educational Research, 70*(2), 181–214.
  - Worked-example research; fading and self-explanation supplements.

## Concept Mapping & Knowledge Graphs

- **Nesbit, J. C., & Adesope, O. O. (2006).** Learning With Concept and Knowledge Maps: A Meta-Analysis. *Review of Educational Research, 76*(3), 413–448.
  - Concept mapping produces small-to-moderate gains. Quality of mapping matters.

- **Novak, J. D., & Cañas, A. J. (2008).** The Theory Underlying Concept Maps. *Practical Assessment, Research & Evaluation, 12*(10), 1–31.

## Adaptive Expertise & Productive Failure

- **Schwartz, D. L., Bransford, J. D., & Sears, D. L. (2005).** Efficiency and Innovation in Transfer. In *Transfer of Learning from a Modern Multidisciplinary Perspective.* IAP.
  - Innovation-before-instruction builds adaptive expertise.

- **Kapur, M. (2008).** Productive Failure. *Cognition and Instruction, 26*(3), 379–424.
  - Failing before instruction outperforms direct instruction on transfer and retention.

- **Kapur, M. (2016).** Examining Productive Failure, Productive Success, Unproductive Failure, and Unproductive Success in Learning. *Educational Psychologist, 51*(2), 289–299.

- **Dreyfus, H. L., & Dreyfus, S. E. (1986).** *Mind Over Machine.* Free Press.
  - 5-stage model of expertise: Novice → Advanced Beginner → Competent → Proficient → Expert.

## Curriculum Design

- **Bruner, J. S. (1960).** *The Process of Education.* Harvard University Press.
  - The spiral curriculum: revisit concepts at increasing depth.

- **Wiggins, G., & McTighe, J. (2005).** *Understanding by Design.* ASCD (2nd ed.).
  - Backward design: start from desired outcomes, work backward to activities.

- **Erickson, H. L. (2002).** *Concept-Based Curriculum and Instruction.* Corwin.
  - Concept-based vs. subject-based curriculum.

- **Spiro, R. J., Feltovich, P. J., Jacobson, M. J., & Coulson, R. L. (1991).** Cognitive Flexibility, Constructivism, and Hypertext. *Educational Technology, 31*(5), 24–33.
  - Cognitive Flexibility Theory. Multiple representations + criss-crossed landscapes for complex knowledge.

- **Meyer, J. H. F., & Land, R. (2003).** Threshold Concepts and Troublesome Knowledge. *Studies in Higher Education, 28*(3), 355–370.
  - Threshold concepts: portals that transform understanding.

## Implementation Intentions

- **Gollwitzer, P. M. (1999).** Implementation Intentions. *American Psychologist, 54*(7), 493–503.
  - If-then plans automate cue → action links.

## Books for Practitioners

- **Ericsson, K. A., & Pool, R. (2016).** *Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise.* Houghton Mifflin Harcourt.
  - Accessible book on deliberate practice.

- **Brown, P. C., Roediger, H. L., & McDaniel, M. A. (2014).** *Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning.* Harvard University Press.
  - Practitioner-friendly synthesis of retrieval, spacing, interleaving.

- **Oakley, B. (2014).** *A Mind for Numbers.* TarcherPerigee.
  - Practitioner-friendly, focuses on STEM learning.

- **Willingham, D. T. (2009).** *Why Don't Students Like School?* Jossey-Bass.
  - Cognitive science of learning, written for educators.
